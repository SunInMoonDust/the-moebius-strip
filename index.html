Hearts and Minds

An intervention.

By John Doe
Fictional Confrontation
~7 min read
Hearts and Minds An intervention. Good morning, Congressman. My name is John Doe. I know you must be busy, so I won’t take up much of your time. I’m a constituent from [your district] and… well… I have a few things I need to get off my chest. I’ll be brief. Most people think of the past as a great engine pushing the present forward. Yet I’ve come to realise it’s the other way around. After watching you and your colleagues in Congress, it’s become painfully obvious that you need an outside perspective. You’ve no doubt heard some version of the maxim, “We will be judged by history.” But it would be closer to the truth to say that we judge history. In other words, what you do and say as a member of Congress has lasting effects on how we—and others—understand our own history. In the past, we Americans were often given the benefit of the doubt. But thanks to you and your colleagues, the opposite is now true. More and more people are rightly questioning our motives — past, present, and future. If I may speak frankly, our distinctly American ideals, dreams, and aspirations are being dismantled and undone by people like you. And for what? You’re probably thinking right now, “So what?”, “Who cares?” But it’s exactly that kind of indifference which is undermining our standing in the world. Because America has always been an idea. An ideal. And our greatest power has always been cultural power. People around the world have looked to us—and looked up to us—as a moral example. Yes, we might be bungling, loud-mouthed, and uncouth, but we meant well and aspired toward justice. After all, it’s part of our national creed: “Liberty and justice for all.” That idea is true power — real and enduring. And once it’s gone, it’s gone forever. Never the same. The genie, so to speak, is out of the bottle for good. To sum up: America’s future prospects depend on how others in the present interpret our past. America and American history are only as great as the perception of goodwill that surrounds us. “I have a dream,” “I cannot tell a lie,” “We the People…” — all of it rings hollow in light of your policies, Congressman. And lest you think this is a partisan attack job, the Democrats are not much better. They are still living in denial about the new political reality ushered in by you and your colleagues. But both of you are missing the point. Politics is not a zero-sum game. It’s not about winning and losing. It’s about reasonable compromise. Yet you seem to believe that you alone are right — and that anyone who dares to disagree with you is the enemy. That’s righteousness, and it is diametrically opposed to good governance. The rest of the world is watching and reconsidering the warnings of people like Charles de Gaulle, Muammar Gaddafi, Saddam Hussein, Osama bin Laden — even Hitler — about the United States. Meanwhile, former allies, faced with random threats and tariffs, are quietly pulling away, keeping their distance, seeking new partnerships. It’s all about hearts and minds, Congressman. Can you even imagine how much credibility and goodwill we’ve lost over the last decade? All of our deeds and misdeeds are being weaponised by enemies and used as a cautionary tale by former friends. Without goodwill, all we have left is guns, bombs, and fast food. And lastly: America used to be the exception — a shining beacon on a hill. Now people see that it was all made of ticky-tacky. As long as there were adults in the room, the rest of us could be forgiven our brashness, crassness, and lack of refinement. Without them, to the rest of the world, we are just vulgar and common. Have you ever seen Eddie Murphy’s Delirious? Well, Congressman, with that in mind: the magic is gone, the philosophy is gone, the context is gone — and all that’s left of America is “Goony-goo-goo!” So let me leave you with this: I wish you much success in this, your era of waning relevance. Good day, Congressman.
